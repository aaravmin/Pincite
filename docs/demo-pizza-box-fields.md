- Technical field / industry:
  Food packaging and food transport containers, specifically single-serve hot-food containers such as pizza boxes.

- Problem solved:
  Standard square pizza boxes trap steam between the pizza and the flat bottom, so the crust turns soggy before it is served.
  The square footprint also wastes material and space around a round pizza.
  Existing vented boxes vent from the top, which does little for moisture trapped beneath the food.

- How it works:
  A round container holds a round pizza with a small bezel of clearance around it.
  Concentric ridges on the base floor lift the pizza off the bottom so steam is not trapped under it.
  Eight ventilation apertures through the base floor give that steam a path out of the container.
  A moisture-channeling feature formed into the underside of the lid collects condensation from above and routes it to the perimeter instead of dripping back onto the food.
  A side hinge and a latch let the container open and close and stay shut in transit.

- Advantages:
  Keeps the crust crisp by removing steam from under and above the food.

### Background
Hot food such as pizza is commonly transported in square corrugated boxes.
A pizza placed on the flat bottom of such a box rests directly on the surface, so steam released by the food is trapped between the food and the bottom.
The trapped steam condenses and is reabsorbed by the crust, which becomes soft and soggy before the food is served.
Square boxes also enclose a round food item inefficiently, wasting material at the corners and taking more shelf and transport space than the food requires.
Prior attempts to address sogginess, such as a single top vent or a small central support, do not remove moisture from beneath the food and only partially address the problem.
A need remains for a container that actively removes moisture from above and below a round hot-food item while using material efficiently.

### Brief summary
A circular container 100 for transporting hot food is disclosed.
The container 100 includes a base 110 having a base floor 112 and an annular base sidewall 118, and a lid 120 having a lid top wall 122 and a lid sidewall 126.
Concentric support ridges 114 on the base floor 112 elevate a food item 200 above the base floor 112 so that steam is not trapped beneath the food item 200.
A plurality of ventilation apertures 116 extends through the base floor 112 to expel the steam from the container 100.
A moisture-channeling feature 124 formed in the underside of the lid 120 collects condensation and directs it toward the perimeter rather than onto the food item 200.
A hinge 130 connects the lid 120 to the base 110, and a latch 140 retains the lid 120 in a closed position.

### Detailed description
Referring to FIG. 1 and FIG. 5, a circular container 100 for transporting hot food includes a base 110 and a lid 120 that together define an enclosed interior sized to receive a round food item 200, such as a personal pizza approximately 14 inches in diameter.
The base 110 and the lid 120 are circular in plan, and the interior is sized so that a bezel ring 150 of approximately 0.5 inch surrounds the food item 200, locating the food item 200 and limiting its movement during transport.
In one embodiment, the base 110 and the lid 120 are molded from recycled fiber, although paperboard or a compostable polymer may be used.

Referring to FIG. 3 and FIG. 6, the base 110 includes a base floor 112 and an annular base sidewall 118 extending upward from a perimeter of the base floor 112.
A plurality of concentric support ridges 114 projects upward from the base floor 112.
The support ridges 114 contact and elevate the food item 200 above the base floor 112, defining a gap between the food item 200 and the base floor 112.
Because the food item 200 is held above the base floor 112, steam released by the food item 200 is not trapped against the underside of the food item 200.
A plurality of ventilation apertures 116 extends through the base floor 112.
In the illustrated embodiment, eight ventilation apertures 116 are arranged around the base floor 112.
The ventilation apertures 116 provide a path by which steam in the gap is expelled from the container 100, so that moisture does not condense beneath the food item 200 and is not reabsorbed by the food item 200.

Referring to FIG. 2 and FIG. 6, the lid 120 includes a lid top wall 122 and a lid sidewall 126 depending from a perimeter of the lid top wall 122.
A moisture-channeling feature 124 is integrally formed in an underside of the lid top wall 122.
Steam that rises within the container 100 condenses on the underside of the lid 120, and the moisture-channeling feature 124 collects the condensation and directs it toward the lid sidewall 126 and the perimeter of the container 100, rather than allowing it to fall back onto the food item 200.

Referring to FIG. 4, the lid 120 is connected to the base 110 by a hinge 130 at one side of the container 100, so that the lid 120 pivots between an open position and a closed position.
A latch 140 at a side of the container 100 opposite the hinge 130 retains the lid 120 in the closed position and resists accidental opening during transport.
A mating rim 128 of the lid 120 seats against a corresponding rim of the base 110 when the container 100 is closed.

In operation, a hot food item 200 is placed on the support ridges 114 and the lid 120 is closed and secured by the latch 140.
During transport, steam from the food item 200 escapes downward through the ventilation apertures 116 and is channeled away from above by the moisture-channeling feature 124, so the food item 200 remains crisp until the container 100 is opened.

### Claims
1. A container for transporting hot food, the container comprising: a circular base having a base floor and an annular sidewall extending from the base floor; a plurality of support ridges projecting from the base floor and configured to elevate a food item above the base floor; a plurality of ventilation apertures extending through the base floor and configured to expel steam from the container; and a circular lid pivotally connected to the base by a hinge and having a moisture-channeling feature formed in an underside of the lid.
2. The container of claim 1, wherein the plurality of ventilation apertures consists of eight apertures arranged around the base floor.
3. The container of claim 1, wherein the plurality of support ridges comprises a plurality of concentric ridges.
4. The container of claim 1, further comprising a latch positioned opposite the hinge and configured to retain the lid in a closed position.
5. The container of claim 1, wherein the base and the lid are formed of molded recycled fiber.
6. The container of claim 1, wherein the lid and the base define an interior sized to receive a round food item with a bezel of about 0.5 inch surrounding the food item.
7. A method of transporting hot food, the method comprising: placing a food item on a plurality of support ridges that elevate the food item above a base floor of a circular container; closing a circular lid onto the base; expelling steam from beneath the food item through a plurality of ventilation apertures in the base floor; and channeling condensation away from the food item with a moisture-channeling feature formed in an underside of the lid.

### Abstract
A circular container transports hot food while keeping it crisp.
A base has a floor with concentric support ridges that elevate the food above the floor and ventilation apertures that expel steam from beneath the food.
A lid has a moisture-channeling feature on its underside that collects condensation and directs it to the perimeter instead of onto the food.
A hinge connects the lid to the base and a latch holds the lid closed during transport.
The base and lid can be molded from recycled fiber, and the round shape uses material efficiently for a round food item.
